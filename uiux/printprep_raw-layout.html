Print Prep

Where MakerBot users can prepare their models to print.

Introduction

After a user creates a 3D model, they go to our cloud-based software to upload their design, change print modes and settings, select a printer, extruder and material type, and add this print to their printer's queue.

Original

In the old version users had to select "display expert settings" to view all settings including quick settings. There was little hierarchy and it didn't have a workflow for either highly technical engineering users or less technical users (e.g. students), when ideally it would work for both well.

Goals

• Increase efficiency by lowering time to prepare print.

• Reduce user-made printing errors by informing users of what print modes will do and organizing settings so only expert users adjust advanced and developer settings.

• Allow users to adjust specific settings on a per model basis.

User Resarch

I led many user interviews and usability tests among core users with various printing applications. Key Takeaways:

• Users aren't understanding that print modes are carefully curated and for most of their needs they don't need to adjust specific settings, which is actually causing errors.

• Users don't immediately know how to change a printer or material, and take a while to find it.

• Users continue to use same specific settings over and over, and have to update those settings every single time and convey them to other team members.

Wireframing

The wireframe containing a model panel, top level information, expanded print modes with custom options, search, extracted move/scale/rotate settings, and high level quick settings.

Final Versions

I restructured settings to have high level quick settings immediately on the right panel, then more settings with categories divided into regular and advanced, with developer settings being its own category. This matches the 4 user personas: beginner, casual, expert, developer.



I brought the printer and material selection to a top navigation since these selections affect other options. Each extruder has different material options, so these needed to be within the same panel. I also wanted users to see all material options so they can get excited about material types, since we found in our research that even if users purchased one printer they bought multiple material types and enjoyed experimented with them.



When a user selects an individual model, in this case through the tree diagram, the unavailable settings for editing a specific model disappear and only per model settings appear.



When a user selects a model, the move/scale/rotate bar appears next to the model tree, and when a user selects one it expands to show these settings. This is the mockup for rotate.

Next Steps

This design is currenly in the process of being implemented, so I haven't been able to collect data on its success. I am collaborating with the product managers to measure speed of print preparation, how often users are printing on a per model basis, and through ongoing user research measure frequency of user-made printing errors based on incorrect settings selection.
